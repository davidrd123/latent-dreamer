The donor-room applause is still moving through the walls of the coatroom, and Eli's text sits open in Tessa's hand like a demand she has not yet answered.
Tessa's thumb finds the credit line on the folded program before she even opens Eli's text — her name, his name, hers listed first — and she reads it the way she might read a verdict she already knew was fair. When she finally looks at the phone, she doesn't read his words so much as begin composing the context he's missing: the room had needed a throughline, someone had to hold the shape of the argument, and she had done that, which is not the same as taking something. The applause is still moving through the wall behind her, and she lets it stay there, lets it mean what it meant a few minutes ago when the meaning was still clean.
Tessa opens the program to the credit line — her name, then the foundation's, then Eli's folded into a cluster of institutional acknowledgments — and she reads it twice as though it might exonerate her on its own, the way a receipt proves a purchase was fair. The applause from the hall is still audible, thin through the coatroom door, and she finds herself composing the reply not as an apology but as a clarification: the toast had to move quickly, donors don't retain compound attributions, she had protected the work by protecting its legibility. The explanation arrives fully formed and reasonable, and she lets it settle before she notices that Eli's name has not appeared in it once.
The keyboard is live now, the unsent draft visible, and the question is no longer whether Tessa will answer but what kind of answer can still count as one.
