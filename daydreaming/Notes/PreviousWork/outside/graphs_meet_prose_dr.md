# When graphs meet prose: the state of bidirectional knowledge-text coupling

**The claim — not the document, not the concept — is emerging as the fundamental atom of structured scholarly knowledge**, and the period from 2023 to 2026 has seen significant convergence across discourse graphs, computational argumentation, digital humanities ontologies, and LLM-powered extraction tools toward systems that treat writing and graph-editing as two views of the same object. True bidirectional synchronization between prose and structured representations remains an unsolved frontier problem, but several research programs and commercial tools are narrowing the gap from both sides. This report maps the landscape across six overlapping areas, highlighting the most consequential work and identifying where the field's structural primitives stand for non-STEM domains.

---

## Joel Chan's discourse graph program sets the pace

The most sustained and coherent research agenda on coupling structured knowledge with prose writing comes from Joel Chan's group at the University of Maryland. Their 2024 paper "Steps Towards an Infrastructure for Scholarly Synthesis" distills three years of research-through-design deploying discourse graph plugins among Roam Research users. The core model represents knowledge as **questions, claims, and evidence** (QUE, CLM, EVD) linked by typed rhetorical relationships (supports, opposes, informs), drawing on earlier formalisms like SWAN, micropublications, and nanopublications.

The most interesting component for bidirectional coupling is the **Discourse Grammar Parser**, which interprets informal prose outlines into formal discourse graph relations. Writers compose naturally in their hypertext notebooks; the parser recognizes patterns in their text that correspond to formal discourse relations and populates the graph automatically. A complementary "Node Menu" lets users formalize informal notes into typed discourse nodes without leaving their writing flow. This represents one of the closest approximations to "prose as a view on structured data" currently deployed in the wild.

The ecosystem has grown beyond a single-tool prototype. Discourse graph plugins now exist for both Roam Research and Obsidian, and the project's conceptualization as a client-agnostic protocol — analogous to Git for knowledge synthesis — enables decentralized push-pull storage of discourse graphs across platforms. Funding from Protocol Labs and Metagov supports ongoing development, with community events running through 2025. Related work from this group includes a UIST 2024 paper on "Patterns of Hypertext-Augmented Sensemaking" (studying 23 scholars' workflows), Creativity & Cognition 2025 papers on immersive sensemaking environments, and the Context24 shared task on contextualizing scientific claims at the Scholarly Document Processing workshop.

At Carnegie Mellon and the Allen Institute for AI, Aniket Kittur and Joseph Chee Chang pursue a complementary agenda. Synergi (UIST 2023) is a mixed-initiative system that uses citation graphs and LLMs to expand and structure research threads that scholars iterate on — a form of coupled structured-and-prose synthesis. Selenite (CHI 2024) scaffolds online sensemaking by having LLMs produce structured overviews of options and criteria that guide subsequent reading. The Semantic Reader Project, described in a 2024 Communications of the ACM article with 45+ co-authors, creates AI-augmented reading interfaces (CiteSee, ScholarPhi, CiteRead) that overlay structured metadata onto prose documents — bridging the reading side of the equation.

## Structural primitives beyond the triple: what atoms fit humanities knowledge

The question of what the right "atoms" are for non-STEM knowledge representation has produced several competing but partially compatible answers in this period.

**Enhanced Rhetorical Structure Theory (eRST)**, published by Amir Zeldes and colleagues in *Computational Linguistics* in early 2025, represents the most significant advance in discourse-level structural primitives. Classical RST constrained discourse relations to tree structures; eRST extends this to **discourse relation graphs** that accommodate tree-breaking, non-projective, and concurrent relations. It introduces over 40 signal types that provide explainable rationales for each discourse relation, organized in a hierarchical taxonomy. A freely available corpus (GUM, 200K+ tokens across 12 genres) supports the framework. For knowledge representation in humanities, eRST matters because it provides a formal, empirically grounded vocabulary for how text segments relate to each other — beyond simple hierarchies — while remaining backwards-compatible with decades of RST-annotated resources.

In computational narratology, the **GOLEM Ontology** (Pannach et al., 2024–2025, published in MDPI *Humanities*) extends CIDOC-CRM and LRMoo into the narratology domain using the DOLCE foundational ontology. GOLEM models narrative structure, character dynamics, and fictional worlds while supporting provenance tracking and pluralistic interpretations. It explicitly engages with Meinong's theory of objects for representing fictional entities via an "as-if-about-only" construct — bridging phenomenological concerns with computational formalism. A related survey on "Narrative Theory-Driven LLM Methods" (arXiv, 2025) maps narratological theories from Genette, Todorov, and cognitive narratology onto LLM capabilities, establishing connections between traditional literary-critical primitives and computational methods.

**Argumentation schemes** continue to evolve as knowledge representation primitives. Walton's scheme framework has been implemented as a knowledge base for multi-agent systems (Ferreira et al., ICEIS 2024), while KR 2024 featured extensive work on formal argumentation including counterfactual explanations, preferential reasoning in ASPIC+, and a position paper on "Contestable AI Needs Computational Argumentation." A comprehensive 2025 survey on LLMs in argument mining documents the shift from supervised pipelines to prompt-driven, retrieval-augmented argumentation analysis.

For scholarly communication specifically, **nanopublications** offer perhaps the most mature atomic primitive. Tobias Kuhn's group at VU Amsterdam continues developing the nanopublication infrastructure — each nanopublication is a small RDF graph consisting of assertion, provenance, and publication information, treated as an independent citable unit. The 2023 field study by Bucur, Kuhn, and Ceolin demonstrated the entire scientific review process (submissions, reviews, responses, decisions) represented as interlinked nanopublications. The ARPHA Writing Tool now allows biodiversity researchers to highlight and export key findings from manuscripts as structured nanopublications inline — a concrete dual-view implementation. An IRCDL 2025 paper proposes extending nanopublications with a fourth "knowledge provenance" component for assertions derived from bodies of evidence rather than single sources, directly connecting to discourse graph concepts.

A notable 2025 ArXiv paper introduces **Knowledge Synthesis Graphs** (KSGs), which use LLMs to extract "Micro-ideas" from collaborative student discourse, link them to "Synthesis Nodes" from course readings, and assign epistemic relations. This represents a direct implementation of LLM-mediated bidirectional coupling between structured knowledge graphs and prose annotation, informed by both discourse graph and argumentation theory.

## The bidirectional sync gap: why text-to-graph works but the loop doesn't close

The central finding of this research landscape is an **asymmetry**: text-to-graph extraction has become mature and commercially viable, while graph-to-text generation is advancing rapidly through LLMs, but **keeping the two synchronized as edits occur remains fundamentally unsolved**. No production system maintains automatic, real-time bidirectional sync between a prose document and a knowledge graph where edits to either propagate to the other.

Microsoft's **GraphRAG** (open-sourced July 2024) exemplifies the state of the art on the extraction side. It uses LLMs to extract entities, relationships, and claims from text corpora, applies community detection via the Leiden algorithm, and builds hierarchical summaries for retrieval. But GraphRAG is a one-way pipeline — it does not regenerate text from graph modifications or propagate graph edits back to source documents. Neo4j's LLM Knowledge Graph Builder (launched June 2024, major 2025 update) similarly operates text-to-graph with provenance links but no reverse channel. Pan et al.'s landmark survey "Unifying Large Language Models and Knowledge Graphs: A Roadmap" (2023–2024) articulates a "Synergized LLMs+KGs" framework where both play equal roles in bidirectional reasoning, but most implementations remain prototypes.

The **Open Research Knowledge Graph (ORKG)** from TIB Hannover comes closest to bidirectional coupling in the scientific domain. Its Reviews feature lets researchers write narrative Markdown alongside inline references to structured ORKG content types — comparisons, contribution data, auto-generated visualizations — all within the same authoring interface. AI-assisted curation using SciBERT and LLMs (GPT-3.5, Llama 2, Mistral) suggests properties for new contributions, while a five-stage human-machine hybrid workflow (SciMantify, 2025) supports annotation. But the sync relies heavily on human curation rather than automated propagation.

**ARTIST** (Huber and Niklaus, INLG 2025), a writing support system from the University of St. Gallen, gets surprisingly close to the dual-view ideal. It analyzes argumentative text and simultaneously displays an interactive argumentation graph showing claims, major claims, and premises, plus an RST parse tree. Users click on graph subgraphs to highlight corresponding text passages. Students using ARTIST wrote essays with **5.1 arguments on average versus 3.2 with baseline tools**, and produced more persuasive writing. However, bidirectionality appears limited — the graph updates from text, but it's unclear whether graph edits propagate back to prose.

**Graphologue** (UIST 2023) suggests one architectural path forward: it converts LLM text responses into interactive node-link diagrams in real-time (97.2% F-score for entity extraction with GPT-4), and users can modify diagrams and request new text through those modifications, creating a partial feedback loop. This LLM-mediated extract-modify-regenerate cycle may be the most viable near-term path to practical bidirectional coupling.

## Commercial research tools are building structured representations, but not graphs writers can edit

The commercial research tool space has evolved significantly since 2023, though the structural representations these tools maintain are primarily **tabular and agent-mediated rather than graph-based and user-navigable**.

**Elicit** (5M+ users, $22M Series A) has moved furthest beyond simple retrieval. Its systematic review workflow automates screening and data extraction across up to 20,000 data points. Research Reports (February 2025) auto-generate customizable briefs following PRISMA structure from up to 80 papers. Most significantly, **Research Agents** (December 2025) represent a shift to agentic AI that conducts multi-step investigations across complex topics, building temporary structured models of research domains. These agents maintain structured internal representations of research landscapes, but users interact with them through prompts and reports rather than navigating or editing the underlying structure directly.

**Consensus** (8M+ users, 220M+ papers) launched its Scholar Agent in 2025 — a multi-agent system with distinct Planning, Search, Reading, and Analysis agents built on GPT-5. Its Research Gaps Heat Map visualizes areas with limited research, and its Results Timeline shows chronological evolution. **Scite.ai** (acquired by Research Solutions) offers the richest structured citation representation: **1.6B+ citations classified as supporting, contrasting, or mentioning** across 280M+ sources, with the Scite Index providing a next-generation ranking metric based on citation context rather than volume.

**Tana** stands out among knowledge management tools as the closest to "writing environment as graph editor." Every note and bullet is a node in a knowledge graph; supertags turn unstructured notes into typed objects (#claim, #question, #source). The outline editor *is* the graph editor — information entered in one view automatically appears in filtered views. **InfraNodus** takes a complementary approach, representing text as networks to identify topical clusters, structural gaps, and trends, with a GPT-powered chatbot that reasons over the graph structure.

**ResearchRabbit** and **Litmaps** merged in October 2025, combining citation-network mapping with semantic search. **Undermind** (Y Combinator-backed) organizes papers into "scientific domain-expert models" — structured, query-specific knowledge models enabling expert-level reasoning. **Iris.ai** raised €7.64M for enterprise R&D workflows with agentic RAG capabilities. None of these tools, however, offer explicit argument maps or discourse graphs that writers can navigate and build upon while composing prose.

## Digital humanities converges on LLM-ontology integration

The intersection of knowledge graphs and LLMs for humanities scholarship has produced its first systematic methodologies. **ATR4CH** (Schimmenti et al., arXiv 2025) provides a five-step methodology for LLM-based knowledge extraction from cultural heritage texts, combining annotation models with CIDOC-CRM, HiCo, and PROV-O ontologies. Applied to authenticity debates about disputed artifacts, it achieved 0.96–0.99 F1 for metadata extraction and 0.65–0.75 F1 for hypothesis extraction. Crucially, it treats annotation, knowledge extraction, and ontology alignment as interdependent processes and explicitly handles scholarly disagreement and multi-perspectival interpretive structures.

The **Pelagios Network** held a landmark Lorentz Center workshop in September 2025 ("Enriching Digital Heritage with LLMs and Linked Open Data"), representing one of the first concrete efforts within a major DH infrastructure project to integrate LLMs with Linked Open Data workflows. A GitHub repository of Python recipes for combining LLMs with LOD for cultural heritage metadata enrichment resulted. The **Semantic Digital Humanities (SemDH) workshops** at ESWC 2024 and 2025 have established a regular venue for this work, with papers on prosopographical knowledge graphs for Irish history, semantic representation of Egyptian demonology, and the Viewsari KG of Vasari's *Lives*.

The **LODinG initiative** (presented at LREC-COLING 2024) explores Linked Open Data across German/Romance philology, Sinology, translatology, and cultural/literary studies, building on the MiMoText knowledge graph for French novels. LLM-assisted ontology building has advanced through frameworks like **Ontogenia** (metacognitive prompting for ontology generation with self-reflection) and **CQbyCQ** (Saeedizade & Blomqvist, 2024), which translates competency questions directly into OWL-compliant schemas. The SEMANTiCS 2024 conference theme — "Knowledge Graphs in the Age of Language Models and Neuro-Symbolic AI" — captured the moment.

Actor-Network Theory approaches to knowledge representation remain more theoretical than implemented. ANT's principles of generalized symmetry and heterogeneous networks map naturally onto graph databases, but no system fully realizes Latourian principles computationally. The closest connections appear in provenance-rich systems like nanopublications and ATR4CH's handling of scholarly disagreement, where attribution and interpretive context are first-class citizens rather than optional metadata.

## Conclusion: five convergences and the missing piece

Five developments are converging toward the vision of prose and graphs as dual views of the same object:

- **Claims as atoms**: Discourse graphs (Chan), nanopublications (Kuhn), argumentation schemes (Walton/Reed), and ORKG all center on **granular, attributed, evidence-linked assertions** as the fundamental unit — not documents, not concepts, not triples. This convergence is the most important structural insight of the period.

- **Graphs over trees**: eRST's move from tree-structured to graph-structured discourse relations, discourse graphs' network models, and property graphs in DH all reflect recognition that humanities knowledge has non-hierarchical, overlapping, multi-perspectival structure that trees cannot capture.

- **Provenance as first-class primitive**: Nanopublications, ATR4CH's handling of scholarly disagreement, HiCo ontology, and GOLEM's support for pluralistic interpretations all treat attribution and interpretive context as essential structural components, not metadata afterthoughts.

- **LLMs as bridge technology**: The Discourse Grammar Parser, Knowledge Synthesis Graphs, ARTIST, ATR4CH, and Graphologue all use language models to mediate between unstructured prose and structured representations — making the extraction/generation loop fast enough to approach interactivity.

- **Platform convergence**: Tana's "outline editor as graph editor," ORKG Reviews' interleaved prose and structured data, and the discourse graph protocol's client-agnostic design all point toward tools where the distinction between writing and knowledge structuring dissolves.

The missing piece remains **automated bidirectional propagation**. Current systems either extract structure from text, generate text from structure, or require manual curation to keep both in sync. The LLM-mediated extract-modify-regenerate cycle (as suggested by Graphologue's architecture) is the most promising near-term path. But making this cycle fast, reliable, and transparent enough that writers experience prose and graph as genuinely two views of one object — rather than two loosely coupled artifacts — remains the field's central unsolved problem. The tools and theories are converging; the engineering to close the loop is what comes next.
