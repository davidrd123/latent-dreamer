# Cognitive scaffolds for LLM-based note structuring: an empirical landscape

**The most important finding across this research: cognitive science frameworks *do* change LLM outputs structurally — but LLMs also exhibit strong gravitational pull toward a default analytical mode that flattens epistemological distinctions between frameworks.** This creates both an opportunity and a core design challenge for any project using these frameworks as computational scaffolds. The opportunity: framework-specific prompting produces measurably different outputs at the procedural level, suggesting real analytical leverage. The challenge: at the inductive/generative level, LLMs converge toward broad, semantic, keyword-based structure regardless of framework instructions — and they systematically overgeneralize, with summaries nearly **5× more likely** to contain broad generalizations than human-authored equivalents. The practical path forward combines DSPy's optimizable pipeline architecture, multi-model disagreement as a quality signal, and careful metrics that detect when structure is being imposed rather than found.

---

## Structure-mapping, force dynamics, and blending: three frameworks at different stages of computational maturity

**Gentner's Structure-Mapping Engine (SME)** is the most computationally mature of the three cognitive frameworks. Falkenhainer, Forbus, and Gentner's 1989 implementation in Common Lisp takes predicate calculus representations of base and target domains, constructs structurally consistent mappings, generates candidate inferences, and identifies breakdown points where mappings fail. Two open-source reimplementations exist: **ANASIME** (Python, github.com/Tijl/ANASIME) includes the full SME algorithm plus two FP-tractable variants, and **SME-clj** (Clojure, github.com/svdm/SME-clj) covers the core mapping machinery. The concrete pipeline: local match hypotheses are generated → structural evaluation scores computed using systematicity-based trickle-down evidence → greedy merging into maximally consistent connected graphs → candidate inferences projected from base to target → breakdown points identified where mapping fails. However, **no one has implemented SME's full machinery as an LLM prompt architecture**. The closest work is a 2025 paper (arXiv:2602.03542) finding that LLMs do not exhibit systematic structure mapping when transferring procedures across representations — a two-stage data curriculum is required to enable even small models to perform generative analogy.

**Talmy's Force Dynamics** has been computationally formalized primarily through Wolff's vector model (2007, *J. Experimental Psychology: General*), which represents CAUSE, ENABLE, and PREVENT as relationships between affector force vectors, patient force vectors, and resultant vectors relative to a target endstate. VerbNet's parser at University of Colorado links verb classes to force-dynamic roles. But **force dynamics has zero implementations as an LLM prompting framework** — this is a completely unexplored space. The agonist/antagonist/tendency/resultant schema has never been operationalized as a prompt template, despite its natural fit for analyzing tensions and dynamics in creative/personal writing.

**Fauconnier and Turner's Conceptual Blending** has the richest computational implementation landscape. The **COINVENT/Amalgamation framework** (Eppe et al., 2018, *Artificial Intelligence*) uses Answer Set Programming via clingo4 and category-theoretic colimits to generate blends — demonstrated on mathematical concept invention and music harmonization. The **Divago system** (Pereira and Cardoso, University of Coimbra) uses genetic algorithms to explore blend space. For LLM integration, **PopBlends** (Wang et al., CHI 2023) is the key empirical result: it compared knowledge-base-driven versus LLM-driven approaches to conceptual blending and found that all three methods provided connections with similar accuracy but **"very different characteristics"** — users found **2× as many blend suggestions** with **half the mental demand**. This is the strongest evidence that different computational frameworks produce structurally different outputs, not just relabeled clusters.

A 2025 paper by Sato (arXiv:2505.10948) operationalizes Conceptual Blending Theory as an experimental framework for LLMs, introducing "Prompt-Induced Transitions" (composition-type blends) and "Prompt-Induced Hallucinations" (elaboration-type blends), though this work is more theoretical than empirically rigorous.

**Critical gap for this project**: no head-to-head comparison exists of structure-mapping versus force dynamics versus conceptual blending as parallel analytical lenses on the same dataset. Whether these frameworks produce genuinely different structure or converge remains an open empirical question — and likely the most valuable contribution this project could make.

## Analogical prompting produces modest gains, but the mechanism may not be analogical

Yasunaga et al.'s "Large Language Models as Analogical Reasoners" (ICLR 2024) introduced analogical prompting: given a problem, the LLM self-generates 3–5 relevant exemplars with solutions, then solves the original problem in a single pass. The prompt structure uses markdown-style headers (`# Relevant problems: Recall three relevant and distinct problems...`). Results across GPT-3.5-turbo, GPT-4, and PaLM2-L showed **average accuracy gains of +4% to +5%** over zero-shot chain-of-thought and manual few-shot CoT, with improvements on GSM8K (elementary math), MATH (competition math), Codeforces (competitive programming, 50 problems), and BIG-Bench Hard tasks.

However, a crucial follow-up — Qin et al. (ACL 2025 Findings), "Relevant or Random: Can LLMs Truly Perform Analogical Reasoning?" — found that **self-generated random examples can achieve comparable or better performance**. On GPT-3.5-turbo: GSM8K scored 75.5% with random examples versus 71.5% with relevant ones; MATH scored 36.1% with random versus 33.3% with relevant. Random *biological* examples boosted GSM8K by ~4% over relevant math examples. Their conclusion: "The accuracy of self-generated examples is the key factor," not relevance per se. This suggests that analogical prompting's benefits come from self-generated chain-of-thought scaffolding rather than genuine analogical transfer — a finding that complicates the theoretical motivation for using cognitive science frameworks but doesn't eliminate the practical value of structured scaffolding.

**Thought Propagation** (Yu et al., ICLR 2024) pushed further, using analogous problems for both solution transfer and plan derivation, achieving **+12% on shortest-path reasoning, +13% human preference in creative writing, and +15% on LLM-agent planning**.

## DSPy 3.x provides the right implementation substrate

**DSPy is at version 3.1.3** (February 2026, ~4.17M monthly downloads) and its architecture maps directly to multi-pass qualitative analysis pipelines. The core abstractions are **Signatures** (declarative I/O specs with Pydantic-compatible typing), **Modules** (composable LM components with a `forward()` method), and **Adapters** (signature-to-prompt translators). Multi-step pipelines compose modules using the standard `forward()` pattern, where each step's output becomes the next step's input with full type safety.

For interpretive tasks, the key modules include `dspy.ChainOfThought` (adds reasoning before output), `dspy.MultiChainComparison` (compares multiple CoT outputs — directly relevant to self-consistency), `dspy.BestOfN` (generates N candidates and selects best), and notably **`dspy.RLM`** (Recursive Language Model for large contexts — see below). A concrete signature for qualitative coding would look like:

```python
class InitialCoding(dspy.Signature):
    """Identify meaningful units and assign initial codes."""
    text: str = dspy.InputField(desc="Raw text to analyze")
    framework: str = dspy.InputField(desc="Analytical framework to apply")
    codes: list[Code] = dspy.OutputField(desc="Codes found in text")
```

The optimizer most suited for interpretive tasks is **GEPA** (Reflective Prompt Evolution, arXiv:2507.19457), which uniquely accepts **text feedback** rather than just scalar scores. It reflects on execution traces to understand *why* scores were low and proposes improved prompts — achieving **22 percentage-point improvements** over vanilla structured outputs on extraction tasks. For subjective metrics, the proven pattern is an **LLM-as-judge** defined as its own DSPy signature, validated against human annotations, then used to optimize the analysis pipeline. Explosion.ai's work with Prodigy-DSPy showed this two-stage approach (optimize the judge first, then optimize the pipeline against the judge) achieves Spearman correlation of ~0.56 with human judgment — far better than BERTScore (0.14) or NER-composite (0.12). **MIPROv2 achieved the highest weighted F1 (0.8248)** on hallucination detection alignment in a comparative study of DSPy optimizers.

No existing DSPy pipelines for qualitative coding or thematic analysis were found — this would be novel work. But the architecture supports it natively, and the GEPA optimizer's text-feedback mechanism directly addresses the "correctness is interpretive" challenge.

## Recursive Language Models solve the context problem for long personal notes

The RLM paradigm, introduced by Alex Zhang, Tim Kraska, and Omar Khattab (MIT CSAIL, arXiv:2512.24601, December 2025), is a **task-agnostic inference approach that addresses context rot** — quality degradation as context length increases. The core insight: instead of feeding all context into a model call, treat context as an external environment variable that the LLM can programmatically inspect, decompose, and recursively process via a Python REPL. The root LM receives only the query (not the full context), writes code to navigate the context variable, and spawns sub-LM calls over selected chunks.

Results are striking: **RLM(GPT-5-mini) outperformed GPT-5 by 34+ points (~114% increase)** on the OOLONG benchmark at 132K tokens, at roughly the same cost. At 263K tokens, it showed **~49% improvement** over GPT-5. On BrowseComp-Plus with 1000+ documents (~5M+ tokens), RLM was the only method maintaining reasonable accuracy. A post-trained **RLM-Qwen3-8B** outperforms base Qwen3-8B by **28.3%** on average, demonstrating the paradigm is trainable.

The critical distinction from Tree-of-Thought and Graph-of-Thought: RLM decomposes *context*, not *reasoning*. ToT explores alternative reasoning paths; GoT models reasoning as a graph with feedback loops; RLM navigates large external contexts programmatically. For a project working with potentially large collections of personal/creative notes, this is directly relevant — and DSPy includes a built-in **`dspy.RLM` module** that integrates RLM into multi-step pipelines.

The official repository (github.com/alexzhang13/rlm, 2,600+ stars, MIT license) supports OpenAI, Anthropic, OpenRouter, and local models via vLLM. A minimal version exists at github.com/alexzhang13/rlm-minimal for building custom implementations.

## Inter-model disagreement is the strongest signal for detecting interpretive imposition

**Self-consistency prompting** (Wang et al., ICLR 2023) samples 40 reasoning paths and takes a majority vote, yielding **+4% to +17.9%** gains on reasoning benchmarks (best on GSM8K). But it requires extractable discrete answers and assumes a single correct answer — fundamentally unsuited for interpretive tasks. **Universal Self-Consistency** (Chen et al., ICML 2024 Workshop) extends this to free-form answers by having the LLM itself judge consistency across candidates, enabling the principle for summarization and open-ended QA.

For interpretive work, **inter-model disagreement is the more powerful signal**. An ICLR 2026 submission shows that cross-model semantic disagreement captures **epistemic uncertainty** that self-consistency misses — specifically flagging confident failures where single-model sampling shows spurious agreement. Using a 5-model ensemble (7–9B instruction-tuned), total uncertainty (aleatoric + epistemic) improved ranking calibration and selective abstention across 10 long-form tasks.

The most revealing empirical data comes from a FAccT 2025 study of moral dilemmas: intra-model self-consistency ranged from α = **0.85–0.89** (Claude, GPT-4) down to α = 0.37 (Llama 2), but **inter-model agreement was dramatically lower** — GPT-3.5 ↔ GPT-4 at α = 0.31, GPT-3.5 ↔ Claude at α = 0.13, with Mistral and Llama 2 showing *negative* agreement with all other models. This is direct evidence that models impose **different systematic interpretive frames** rather than converging on robust interpretations.

A **Confidence-Diversity Calibration** framework (arXiv:2508.02029) formalizes this as a dual-signal quality metric combining LLM self-reported confidence with inter-model diversity (entropy). On accessible tasks, it achieves R² = **0.979** predicting inter-coder agreement. Cross-domain validation on 2,035 cases showed improvement of **6.6–113.7%** over single-signal baselines. However, the authors caution that "exceptional R² values may not generalize to more complex interpretive tasks."

LLMs are **universally overconfident**: ECE ranges from 0.12 to 0.57 across studies. Verbalized confidence clusters between 80–100% regardless of accuracy. A striking finding from KalshiBench (arXiv:2512.16030): extended reasoning **worsens** calibration — models become more confident without becoming more accurate. Human superforecasters achieve ECE ~0.03–0.05; LLMs manage only 0.12–0.40.

## When LLMs do qualitative coding, frameworks matter procedurally but converge epistemologically

The **"Scaling Hermeneutics"** paper (Dunivin, *EPJ Data Science*, April 2025) establishes the empirical baseline. Using 9 binary socio-historical codes applied to news passages about W.E.B. Du Bois, GPT-4 achieved Cohen's **κ ≥ 0.79** (excellent agreement) for 3 of 9 codes and κ ≥ 0.6 (substantial) for 8 of 9. Chain-of-thought prompting improved mean κ from 0.59 to **0.68**; decomposed coding (one code per prompt) similarly improved from 0.60 to 0.68. GPT-3.5 was dramatically worse (mean κ = 0.34). The paper proposes "reflexive content analysis" — humans retain interpretive leadership over codebook development while LLMs apply codes at scale.

Multiple teams have adapted **Braun and Clarke's reflexive thematic analysis** for LLMs, with convergent findings:

- **DeTAILS** (Sharma et al., 2025, University of Waterloo): 18 qualitative researchers using an LLM-assisted RTA toolkit. Critical finding — although designed for reflexive TA, the system **naturally aligned more with codebook TA** due to the stabilized codebooks needed at scale. Novices over-trusted fluent outputs.
- **LATA** (Wang et al., ACM CSCW 2025): GPT-4 produced codes as **keywords and concepts** rather than the **"ideas"** that RTA demands — a fundamental epistemological mismatch. Cosine similarity with human analysis reached 0.76.
- **Vikan et al.** (2025, *Qualitative Health Research*): "At this stage, base LLMs provide limited support and do not increase the efficiency of RTA." LLMs could not interpret underlying cultural, situated, and contextual meanings.

The emerging synthesis on the key question — whether different frameworks produce genuinely different structure — points to a **two-level answer**. At the **deductive/application level** (applying existing codebooks), framework choice matters because it shapes the codebook structure, and LLMs faithfully execute different coding schemas. Procedural-level instructions ("code item-by-item using constant comparison") produce meaningfully better results than generic instructions. At the **inductive/generation level** (generating themes from scratch), LLMs converge toward a default mode that is semantic rather than latent, broad rather than nuanced, keyword-based rather than idea-based, and consistent rather than reflexive. This default mode resembles codebook thematic analysis **regardless of whether the prompt specifies grounded theory, reflexive TA, or content analysis**. The deeper epistemological distinctions between frameworks are largely flattened.

Performance varies enormously by construct type: **sentiment analysis** consistently achieves κ = 0.91–0.95, while evidence of impact and metacognitive thinking are weakest. Non-mainstream discourse is especially problematic — a study of Black girls' computational making discourse showed significantly low agreement across all codes even with detailed definitions. **No papers were found on LLM qualitative coding of personal or creative texts specifically** — this is a genuine gap.

## LLM summaries overgeneralize, and no reliable metric yet distinguishes found from imposed structure

Peters and Chin-Yee's Royal Society paper (*Royal Society Open Science*, 12(4), 2025) tested **10 LLMs** on **4,900 summaries** of scientific texts (100 multidisciplinary abstracts, 100 medical abstracts, 100 full clinical articles). They measured three types of overgeneralization: generic generalizations (dropping quantifiers), present tense generalizations (shifting from past to present tense), and action-guiding generalizations (converting descriptions to recommendations). **LLM summaries were nearly 5× more likely** to contain broad generalizations than NEJM Journal Watch human-authored summaries (odds ratio = **4.85**, 95% CI [3.06, 7.70], p < 0.001). DeepSeek, ChatGPT-4o, and LLaMA 3.3 overgeneralized in **26–73% of cases**. Counterintuitively, prompting models to avoid inaccuracies made them **nearly twice as likely** to produce overgeneralized conclusions. Claude showed consistently the lowest overgeneralization rates.

Simon Willison's **LLM CLI tool** (v0.23+) represents the state of the art in practical structured extraction, with a concise schema DSL (`llm --schema 'name,age int,short_bio'`), cross-provider support, content-hashed schema reuse, and SQLite-backed querying of all prior extractions. The critical design observation: his tools treat extraction (finding structure in data) and generation (imposing structure onto data) identically — the schema constrains form, not provenance. This is true of all major structured output frameworks (Instructor, Outlines, Guidance, vLLM): **structural conformance ≠ extraction faithfulness**. A tool can guarantee valid JSON matching a schema while the LLM fills fields with hallucinated content.

No established metric currently exists for systematically distinguishing "found" from "imposed" structure. Wang et al. (2025, arXiv:2511.07318) demonstrate that spurious-correlation-driven hallucinations are immune to model scaling, evade confidence-based detection, and persist after refusal fine-tuning. The most promising detection approaches identified across the literature are:

- **Source comparison**: Systematically comparing extracted claims to source text (as Peters and Chin-Yee did for overgeneralization)
- **Multi-model consensus**: Running extraction across multiple models and treating divergence as an imposition signal
- **Counterfactual testing**: Presenting shuffled or randomized data and checking if the same structure emerges
- **Confidence-diversity calibration**: Combining self-reported confidence with inter-model entropy as a dual-signal quality metric

## Conclusion: a concrete architecture emerges

The research converges on a specific implementable architecture. **DSPy 3.x** provides the pipeline substrate — typed signatures for each analytical pass, composable modules, and GEPA optimization against LLM-as-judge metrics. The three cognitive science frameworks should be implemented as **parallel deductive codebooks** (where they demonstrably produce different outputs) rather than parallel inductive generation prompts (where LLMs converge). Structure-mapping focuses on systematic correspondences and candidate inferences between domains; force dynamics captures agonist/antagonist tensions and resultant dynamics; conceptual blending identifies input spaces and emergent structure in the blend. Each framework should produce typed structured output via Pydantic models, enabling direct comparison.

**Inter-model disagreement** (not self-consistency) is the right quality signal for interpretive tasks — multiple models running the same framework, or the same model running multiple frameworks, with divergence measured as a proxy for interpretive imposition versus robust finding. The confidence-diversity calibration framework provides a validated dual-signal metric. Counterfactual controls (running the same pipeline on shuffled text) can detect apophenia. For large note collections, **RLM** (available as `dspy.RLM`) solves the context-length problem without information loss from summarization.

The most novel contribution this project can make is the head-to-head comparison no one has yet performed: do structure-mapping, force dynamics, and conceptual blending produce genuinely different analytical structure when applied to the same personal/creative texts? The PopBlends result (structurally different outputs from different approaches at similar accuracy) suggests yes, but the qualitative coding literature's finding of epistemological flattening suggests the difference may be smaller than theory predicts. Measuring this gap — and building a pipeline that maximizes genuine analytical divergence while detecting convergence-as-imposition — is where the real empirical contribution lies.
