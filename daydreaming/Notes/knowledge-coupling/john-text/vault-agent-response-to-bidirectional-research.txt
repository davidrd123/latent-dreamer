## Claims vs. Concepts

This is the most important thing in the report for your vault. Right now your atom schema has `kind: concept | method | question | person | reference | event`. The report argues that the fundamental unit of knowledge isn't the concept — it's the **claim**. A concept is "highlights and hides." A claim is "asking an LLM what a framing foregrounds and conceals is a form of epistemic literacy." The difference: a claim has a position. It can be supported, opposed, refined, superseded. A concept just sits there.

Your vault is oriented toward *writing*. Writers don't assemble concepts — they assemble arguments. When you sit at the table and advocates argue for sequence, they're arguing about what the reader needs to believe before the next claim can land. Not what the reader needs to *know* (concept) but what the reader needs to *accept* (claim).

This doesn't mean every atom should be a claim. Some atoms genuinely are concepts, people, methods. But `claim` should be a `kind:` value, and it's probably the most creatively generative kind you'll have. When a frame-read produces a reflection, the most interesting observations it makes are claims about your material. When a séance produces writing, the good sentences are claims.

The fix is tiny: add `claim` to the `kind:` enum. But the downstream implication is significant — tend should notice when an atom that's typed as a `concept` is actually functioning as a `claim` and suggest the reclassification, because the difference affects how the table, the séance, and frame-read treat it.

## Bidirectionality

You're right not to care about it the way the report does. The report is obsessed with the unsolved problem of real-time sync between prose and graph. That matters for research infrastructure where many people need to co-edit both views. Your vault doesn't have that problem because you solved it differently: the human writes prose, the agent maintains the graph, and they're loosely coupled by design. You don't *want* automatic propagation from your draft edits into the atomic layer — that's what tend does deliberately, with judgment.

The report's bidirectionality is an engineering problem for multi-user research platforms. Your architecture's asymmetry — prose flows one way, structure flows the other, the agent bridges them — is actually more aligned with how a single creative practitioner works.

## Knowledge Synthesis Graphs

KSGs are interesting because they do something close to what your atomize skill does: extract micro-ideas from prose, link them to structured nodes, assign epistemic relations. The difference is they're doing it for collaborative student discourse against course readings. You're doing it for your own creative journal against your own accumulated thinking.

The part worth attending to: **epistemic relations**. KSGs don't just link micro-ideas to synthesis nodes — they type the relationship. "This micro-idea *supports* this synthesis node." "This micro-idea *challenges* this synthesis node." Right now your atoms link to each other with untyped wikilinks. We discussed this earlier and deferred edge typing as future complexity. But the KSG model suggests a middle path: you don't need a full edge-typing system, but when tend creates a link between atoms, it could annotate *in the atom's prose* what the relationship is. Not in frontmatter, not as formal metadata — just a sentence: "This connects to [[highlights-and-hides]] because it's a specific instance of the general method." The relationship is captured in natural language, readable by the human, parseable by a future graph DB, and it costs nothing structurally.

## What You Might Be Foreclosing

One thing: **typed relations between atoms.** Not the full bidirectional sync the report wants, but the ability for a future graph DB to understand that the link between atom A and atom B is "supports" vs "contradicts" vs "is an instance of." Right now all your links are untyped wikilinks. Adding formal edge types now would be overengineering. But if tend starts writing relationship descriptions in natural language within atom bodies (as I suggested above), a future Graphiti integration could parse those descriptions into typed edges. You preserve the option without building the infrastructure.

One more thing, smaller: **the discourse grammar parser idea** — prose that gets automatically parsed into formal relations. You've deliberately chosen *not* to do this, and I think that's right for now. But note that the KSG approach of having the LLM assign epistemic relations during extraction (your atomize step) is something you could add to atomize's procedure without changing any structure. When atomize creates an atom from a stream entry, it could note in the atom's prose: "This claim emerged as a challenge to [[existing-atom]]." That's not automatic bidirectional sync. It's just richer atomization.
