Yes. And the right way to widen the net is to stop treating all these sources as doing the same job.

There are really four jobs here:

1. **seed the inner life**
   what is charged, denied, unresolved, haunting, attractive

2. **update it causally**
   what changes concern strength, operator choice, and memory

3. **segment and store experience**
   where events begin/end, what gets remembered, what gets rewritten

4. **realize it in language**
   how agency, focus, and stance get rendered

Your opus is mostly right. The main correction is: **Freud, Jung, narrative identity, and IFS are better for 1 than 2. Attachment, appraisal, control, self-discrepancy, event cognition, and some predictive-processing work are better for 2 and 3. Cognitive linguistics is strongest at 4, and secondarily at constrained content synthesis.**

## What I would keep from the wider net

### Freud: yes, but upstream

Freud is still useful, just not where people usually want him.

The good steals are:

* **defense-like operator families**
  avoidance, rationalization, reversal, displacement-like redirection
* **overdetermination**
  a good product serves several pressures at once
* **secondary revision**
  raw inner material gets smoothed into narratable sequence after the fact

Mueller already harvested the first and third of those, and explicitly elevates overdetermination as unfinished business. So Freud is not some exotic extra here. He is already in the blood of the architecture.

What I would **not** do is turn Freudian metapsychology into runtime ontology. You do not need libido hydraulics, id/ego/superego machinery, or dream-symbol dictionaries in the kernel.

### Jung: useful, but not causal

Jung is good for:

* **resonance notes**
* **shadow tags**
* **complexes as triggerable bundles**
* **archetypal pressure templates for the authoring bible**

He is weak as a causal update model.

So I would use Jung for **what kind of material is charged**, not for **how the system should update state**. "Shadow" is a great authoring-time tag. It is not a good equation.

### James: dashboard gold, kernel maybe not

James is still good for phenomenology: focus, fringe, undertow, stream. That is dashboard material. It helps you decide what the audience should see of a mind.

But James is not the place I would go for concern update rules. He helps you present the stream; he does not give you the best mechanics for running it.

## The strongest causal imports, outside Mueller

### Attachment theory is stronger than people realize

Bowlby is not just "attachment style." The core thing is that attachment is a **motivational control system** regulating proximity to an attachment figure. Fraley's overview states Bowlby explicitly framed it as a behavioral system, and even reduces it to a question: is the attachment figure nearby, accessible, and attentive? If yes, exploration opens up. If no, anxiety and proximity-seeking behaviors activate. ([Psychology Department Labs][1])

That maps almost embarrassingly well to your architecture:

* concern activation
* exploration vs protest
* approach/avoid oscillation
* repair-seeking
* despair after failed re-contact

I would import attachment not mainly as four boxed "styles," but as:

* **anxiety**
* **avoidance**
* **repair expectation**
* **accessibility prior**

Those are usable parameters.

### Predictive processing / active inference is a good discipline, not a replacement

There really is causal modeling of emotion here. Seth's interoceptive-inference review describes emotion as arising within hierarchical inference over bodily and environmental states, with bodily regulation enslaved to top-down predictions. Smith et al. go even more concrete, modeling emotion concepts as hidden states that generate interoceptive, exteroceptive, and proprioceptive consequences, with selective attention and policy choice built into inference. ([PMC][2])

For your system, the useful steal is not "replace Mueller with Friston." It is:

* concern intensity as **prediction-error magnitude**
* urgency as **expected error growth**
* operator choice as **error-reduction strategy**
* attention shifts as **precision control**
* reappraisal as **model revision rather than decorative rewrite**

That gives you a principled story for why rehearsal, reversal, roving, and rationalization change felt pressure.

### Self-discrepancy theory is a very good concern extractor

Higgins is one of the cleanest imports for "why this particular feeling, now?"

The basic claim is:

* **actual vs ideal** discrepancies track **dejection-type** emotion
* **actual vs ought** discrepancies track **agitation-type** emotion

The later review also says these effects depend on magnitude, accessibility, relevance in the current context, and importance. That is exactly the kind of machinery you want for concern extraction from authored primitives. ([PubMed][3])

This is a nice bridge from narrative contradiction to typed pressure:

* failed aspiration
* violated duty
* shame/guilt/agitation/dejection splits
* context-sensitive activation

### Event segmentation belongs much closer to the core than people think

Zacks' event segmentation work says people automatically parse continuous activity into meaningful events, and that those boundaries scaffold later memory and learning. ([PMC][4])

That matters because your system needs to know:

* when a candidate becomes a node
* what counts as one event versus aftermath
* what gets indexed together
* when the dashboard should visibly shift state

So event segmentation is not just narration polish. It is part of graph compilation and episodic storage.

### The interpersonal circumplex is a very good low-dimensional social field

The interpersonal circumplex gives you two broad axes:

* **affiliation** (warmth vs hostility)
* **control** (dominance vs submissiveness)

It also gives you complementarity predictions: warmth tends to invite warmth, dominance tends to invite submissiveness, and so on. That is exactly the kind of low-dimensional social geometry that can bias practices and operator choice without forcing a giant ontology. ([PMC][5])

I think this is a better import than many people realize. It is cheap, interpretable, and it connects relation state to likely next moves.

## Cognitive linguistics: yes, but mostly in two places

### Conceptual blending is very relevant

Fauconnier and Turner are not fluff here. Conceptual blending is the selective projection of structure from input spaces into a blended space, where completion and elaboration create emergent structure not present in either input alone. ([ScienceDirect][6])

That is a very good description of what a bounded LLM call should be doing inside your pipeline:

* input space A: current causal slice / situation
* input space B: retrieved episode cluster
* operator: what kind of integration is allowed
* output: a blended candidate moment

So yes, conceptual blending belongs in the architecture, but not as the whole architecture. It is a good model of the **content-synthesis step inside an operator boundary**.

### Cognitive grammar / construction grammar is strongest downstream

Langacker's core claim is that meaning is conceptualization and grammar is not separate from semantic construal. ([CSE IIT Kanpur][7])

That means narration choices are not neutral:

* "Kai avoided the letter"
* "The letter sat unopened"
* "The letter remained where he left it"

Those are different construals of agency and attention.

There is even experimental evidence that language framing changes attention and memory for agency, especially around accidents. Fausey and colleagues found cross-linguistic differences in agentive framing, and manipulating linguistic agency shifted eyewitness memory. ([PMC][8])

So cognitive linguistics is not just pretty talk. It directly informs:

* dashboard narration
* stance selection
* agency highlighting or muting
* possibly even how users remember what the system showed them

## Narrative identity and parts models

### McAdams is a very clean fit

McAdams' actor / agent / author framing is unusually compatible with your stack:

* **actor** = social surface and observable role
* **agent** = goals, motives, concerns
* **author** = retrospective life-story organization over time

That gives you a principled way to think about persistent daemons, long-horizon coherence, and why some generated moments feel like "this belongs to this mind." ([PubMed][9])

I would use narrative identity mainly at:

* curation time
* accepted-episode accumulation
* long-horizon memory shaping
* daemon-style companionship

Not as the low-level scene generator.

### IFS is a good UI lens, not a kernel ontology

IFS, on its own official framing, is a parts-based model of mental life with distinct subparts and a "Self" relation to them. ([Internal Family Systems][10])

For you, the best use is:

* dashboard vocabulary
* author-facing diagnostics
* conflict visualization

I would not make every concern a permanent ontological part. That gets baroque fast. Better to treat "parts" as **temporary bundles or recurring clusters of concerns and operator biases**.

## What your opuses are really pointing at

You're right that the project framing may be too narrow.

The performance-instrument framing is real. Keep it. But the mechanisms themselves are more general.

A Mueller-shaped system is not just "for watched daydreams." It is a candidate architecture for **persistent cognitive sidecars**:

* writing companions
* reading companions
* research daemons
* vault agents with evolving preoccupations

The big distinction is this:

**Memory systems answer questions about what they know.
Concern systems generate questions about what they still care about.**

That is the difference between:

* vector DB + retrieval
* and a daemon that keeps coming back to something because it still has unresolved pressure around it

That is a real product gap.

## Why this still has not been built in a serious way

I think there are two kinds of reasons.

### 1. The old technical blockers were brutal

Mueller himself named most of them: too few rules/episodes to hit critical mass, brittleness of exact matching/unification, slowness, heavy hand-tailoring, and no scalable acquisition path for rich content. Those are real reasons the original program did not become the seed of a modern ecosystem.

LLMs, embeddings, and modern retrieval help with **content supply** and **semantic fuzziness**. They do **not** by themselves solve:

* persistent concern competition
* typed operator control
* explicit hypothetical vs committed distinction
* curation
* graph compilation

So the stack is finally feasible, but it is still not trivial.

### 2. Most current agent products optimize for helpfulness, not cognitive texture

This matters.

Task agents are optimized to:

* answer correctly
* plan efficiently
* complete tasks
* not get weird

A Mueller-shaped sidecar is optimized to:

* keep unresolved tensions alive
* notice recurrences
* reinterpret earlier material
* surface surprising cross-links
* sometimes obsess a little, in a productive way

For customer support, that is a bug.
For writing, reading, research, and companionship, that may be the product.

That is why your daemon intuition is right.

## My sharper take on what to build from all this

If I compress the wider net into actual design pressure, I get this:

**For the kernel**

* attachment-style accessibility priors
* self-discrepancy concern extraction
* prediction-error flavored intensity / reappraisal
* event segmentation for storage and graph compilation
* interpersonal circumplex as low-dimensional social field

**For authoring-time semantics**

* Freud for operator semantics and overdetermination
* Jung for resonance/shadow tagging
* McAdams for long-horizon coherence
* IFS as a human-readable dashboard lens

**For realization**

* conceptual blending inside operator-bounded LLM calls
* cognitive/construction grammar for agency and stance in narration

That is a much cleaner map than "psychology, generally."

## And on your creative-helper-daemon point

Yes, I think that is real.

A persistent daemon over your notes/KB gets much more interesting if it has:

* recurring concerns
* episodic memory across sessions
* decay
* serendipitous reactivation
* operator-specific thinking modes
* explicit hypothetical vs committed status
* user-visible provenance and veto

Then it stops being "chat with a large scratchpad" and starts becoming:

* a reader that develops suspicions
* a writer's companion that ruminates
* a research assistant that accumulates live hypotheses
* a vault sidecar that notices multi-month patterns

The caution is obvious: if you do this badly, you get decorative inner life, fake obsession, and sticky nonsense. So the discipline has to be:

* transparent traces
* editable concern state
* controllable salience
* no silent mutation of canon or KB
* bounded operator families
* decay and dismissal that actually work

That is why the architecture matters. It gives the daemon a mind-shaped control loop instead of a memory-shaped facade.

If I had to pick the **five highest-yield non-Mueller imports** from this wider net, I would pick:

1. attachment/control-system theory
2. self-discrepancy + reappraisal
3. event segmentation
4. interpersonal circumplex
5. conceptual blending

Those five would change behavior the most, with the least theory cosplay.
