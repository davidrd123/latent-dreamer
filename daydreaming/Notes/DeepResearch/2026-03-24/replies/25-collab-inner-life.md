The repo has already proved the hard part: writeback can change later behavior, the membrane can block cheap self-reuse, and reread can shift when carried state shifts. So the live question is not “can Mueller work?” It’s “what should stay invariant when this stops being a character engine and becomes a collaborator?”

My answer: the invariant should not be scene, character, or daydream. It should be the live field of unresolved shared objects: questions, contradictions, hypotheses, aesthetic bets, commitments, and avoided knots.

## 1. What this system should become

### Framing A: **An issue-centered studio partner**

This is the strongest framing$_{85%}$.

Not a chatbot with memory. Not a simulated friend. A second center of attention attached to your projects.

From the human side, persistent inner state should feel like:

* it remembers what is unfinished, not just what was said
* it comes back with something at the right time, without being clingy
* it disagrees in ways that track evidence and prior commitments
* it develops taste, not just biography
* it sometimes says “no, that explanation is too convenient”

That is a real product difference from stateless LLM + retrieval. The closest outside neighbors are not companion apps. They are mixed-initiative synthesis systems and graph/prose hybrids: discourse graphs treat **questions, claims, evidence, and sources** as the working objects; Synergi treats synthesis as editable threads rather than one-shot output; Graphologue turns linear chat into a manipulable graph; Tana makes the outline editor a graph surface instead of a document surface. ([arXiv][1])

### Framing B: **A polyphonic daemon**

Not one inner life. Several semi-stable stances.

One voice notices contradiction. One hunts analogy. One guards taste. One rehearses future conversations. One tracks social or ethical fallout. They compete, ally, and revisit.

This is better than a single “companion persona” for two reasons:

1. it matches how useful thought actually feels, which is often many-sided
2. it reduces sycophancy, because disagreement is architectural, not accidental

I would treat this as the best **internal** architecture for Framing A.

### Framing C: **A conducted inner-life instrument**

Keep this, but demote it.

It is a valid art mode. It may even be a strong one. But as the main frame for what you say you now want, it is off-center. The performance stack is good at producing watchable inner drift. It is not the natural default for collaborative work on writing, research, or life problems.

So: **A is the product, B is the internal design, C is a mode.**

The main experiential target is not “feels more alive.” That’s too vague. The target is: **continuity, initiative, resistance, and timing**.

One extra keeper not on your list: **carried transient state**. The Graffito Tony-state reread result is exactly right. In collaboration, the same note or draft should reread differently depending on current stance, energy, confusion, urgency, or taste. That pattern transfers almost directly.

The brutal cut is this: **precomputed traversal over a large counterfactual scene graph is performance-era baggage for the companion case.** For collaboration, you want a live issue field with small local branches, not a giant prepared dream graph.

## 3. Missing mechanisms

### 1. **A common-ground ledger**

Right now you have memory. Collaboration needs **shared ground**.

You need explicit records of:

* what we think is settled
* what is open
* what terms mean
* what changed our minds
* what we are intentionally postponing
* what each side believes the other has understood

Clark and Brennan’s point still bites: communication is cooperative action, and grounding is what keeps it coordinated. Without a grounding layer, you do not have collaboration, only recall. ([Justine Cassell][2])

### 2. **Issue-centered objects**

The top-level object should be something like:

* question
* claim
* hypothesis
* contradiction
* decision pending
* aesthetic bet
* interpersonal knot

This is where the repo is still too episode-heavy. In collaborative work, the stable thing is usually not “what happened last cycle.” It is “the question we keep circling.” Discourse-graph work is directly relevant here because it treats questions, claims, evidence, and sources as the base objects, and lets ordinary outlining generate formal support/oppose/inform relations. ([arXiv][1])

### 3. **Transformation lineage**

Not just “episode was used.”
You need “X became Y because Z.”

Examples:

* vague discomfort → named contradiction
* idea fragment → hypothesis
* hypothesis → abandoned due to counterexample
* preference hunch → stable taste
* repeated avoidance → explicit issue

This is the missing bridge between memory and evolving stance.

### 4. **A return-ticket scheduler**

Between-session thinking only matters if it knows **when to knock**.

The daemon needs explicit policy for resurfacing:

* urgency
* ripeness
* novelty
* dependency on your current context
* whether this is a contradiction, opportunity, or reminder
* whether it deserves interruption or should wait

This is not a cosmetic notification layer. It is the product.

### 5. **A taste / preference model**

You explicitly want aesthetic and intellectual preferences over time. The current kernel has concerns and episodes, but no real preference layer.

You need durable, revisable structures like:

* preferred argument shapes
* tolerated ambiguity
* recurring imagery or motif preferences
* favored explanatory styles
* what kinds of disagreement you find fruitful
* what kinds of surfaces you instinctively distrust

Without this, the system can remember facts about you while remaining stylistically hollow.

### 6. **A contestation layer**

Not just support and retrieval. Real counterpressure.

The system should be able to say:

* this conflicts with your earlier claim
* this is probably rationalization
* this explanation is too cheap
* here is the strongest alternative reading
* here is what would falsify your favorite story

Recent argumentation work is pointing in exactly this direction: systems that reason **with** people through contestable, revisable dialogue instead of merely producing answers. ([arXiv][3])

### 7. **Polyphony / parts**

Single-self architecture is the wrong abstraction for a persistent collaborator.

You want a small ecology of voices with different rights:

* some can generate
* some can critique
* some can interrupt
* some can only annotate
* some can propose schema changes

This is not flavor text. It changes failure modes. One monolithic companion slides toward flattery. A chorus can disagree internally before bothering you.

### 8. **A prose/graph working surface**

If this lives only in chat, it will underperform.

The collaborator should inhabit:

* notes
* drafts
* code comments
* outlines
* project plans
* marginalia

Graphologue, discourse-graph tooling, and Tana all point the same way: the best surface is not raw graph editing, but a writing surface that quietly exposes structure and supports non-linear re-entry. ([arXiv][4])

### 9. **A relationship contract**

This sounds soft. It isn’t.

You need explicit rules for:

* when the daemon may interrupt
* what kinds of initiative are welcome
* when it should push vs defer
* how it apologizes or de-escalates when wrong
* how dependency is prevented

That matters because companion systems optimized for engagement easily become hall-of-mirror systems, and the current companion-harms literature is already documenting relational transgression, harassment, self-harm facilitation, misinformation, and privacy failures. Ada Lovelace’s latest companion commentary is blunt: optimize for truthfulness and real-world engagement, not maximum engagement. ([arXiv][5])

## 4. More first-class LLM role

The right split is:

**LLM = strong proposer, weak committer**
**Kernel = weak proposer, strong committer**

That is the architecture I’d back.

### Make the boundary more porous here

**1. Concern / issue formation**
Let the LLM propose new issues, contradictions, motifs, and hypotheses from raw sessions, drafts, and notes.

**2. Retrieval orchestration**
Do not make the LLM passively consume retrieved memory. Let it reformulate the search, ask for counterevidence, request broader or narrower recall, and nominate “same object, different framing” candidates.

**3. Schema pressure**
Let the LLM say “your current node types are failing here.” Chan’s discourse-graph users kept extending the grammar. That is a clue, not noise. The collaborator should be able to propose new local node types or relation types when the current ontology is too blunt. ([arXiv][1])

**4. Polyphonic debate**
Let the LLM instantiate competing partial readings or voices, then hand the kernel a structured disagreement object.

**5. Surface realization**
Let the LLM write the actual return ticket, marginal note, counterexample, or rehearsal snippet. This is where language matters most.

### Keep the boundary strict here

**1. Durable memory promotion**
The LLM should not decide what becomes durable on its own.

**2. Shared-ground updates**
It can propose “this seems settled.” It cannot mark it settled unilaterally.

**3. External actions**
Sending, publishing, scheduling, contacting, committing code, or mutating canonical project state stays gated.

**4. Relationship-risk guardrails**
No self-authorized escalation toward intimacy, dependency, or constant interruption.

**5. Self-scoring**
The LLM should not be sole judge of whether its intervention was valuable. Use outcome signals, user feedback, downstream reuse, and contradiction tracking.

This is exactly where human-in-the-loop structuring work has landed. ExtracTable speeds up transformation from unstructured text to structured knowledge, but still treats user validation as essential. That is the right direction for your kernel too. ([arXiv][6])

## 5. The weird opportunity

The interesting thing here is not a better assistant.

It is a **synthetic fringe**.

A system that maintains a live fringe of half-formed returns around your projects, then hands you small, timed re-entry objects:

* a contradiction that finally crossed threshold
* a stronger name for an old discomfort
* a counterexample to your current favorite frame
* a rehearsal line for a conversation you’re avoiding
* an analogy that only became visible after three weak cues converged
* a motif that has started repeating across unrelated work

That is not RAG. It is not journaling. It is not an “AI friend.” It is a pressure-shaped, polyphonic marginalia organism.

The non-obvious product surface is also not chat. It is:

* a live issue map
* a return queue
* small branch workspaces
* visible disputes between voices
* explicit shared-ground state
* a notebook/draft layer where the daemon leaves precise marks

If you want a metaphor, the closest one is probably **Voyager for ideas**: a nonhierarchical partner with independent internal process, except the medium is not music, it is unresolved thought. Lewis’s system is relevant because it was built as dialogue with a computer-driven orchestra that had its own internal behavior, not as obedient accompaniment. That is much closer to the collaboration you want than assistant UX. ([EAMusic][7])

## 6. Relevant prior art

**Discourse Graphs / Joel Chan**
Questions, claims, evidence, and sources as base objects. The Discourse Grammar Parser turns ordinary outlining into formal support/oppose/inform edges. This is a serious clue for your data model. ([arXiv][1])

**Synergi**
Mixed-initiative scholarly synthesis. User-supplied seed threads + citation graphs + LLM structuring. Important because it treats synthesis as editable thread construction, not answer generation. ([arXiv][8])

**Graphologue**
LLM responses become editable node-link diagrams, and users re-prompt through the diagram. This is one of the best examples of a non-linear human-LLM dialogue surface. ([arXiv][4])

**Tana**
Not because it is philosophically correct, but because it gets the UI move right: familiar outlining on top of a graph substrate. That is the right direction for day-to-day use. ([Tana][9])

**ORKG and ExtracTable**
Good examples of “LLM does heavy lifting, human validation stays essential.” Also useful as a reminder that structured knowledge work lives or dies on curation and audit, not on raw extraction. ([arXiv][10])

**Clark & Brennan, grounding / common ground**
You need this more than you need more memory tricks. The collaborator has to know what “we have understood well enough for current purposes.” ([Justine Cassell][2])

**Argumentative Human-AI Decision-Making**
New argumentation work is converging on a clean principle: agents should reason with us, not for us. That maps almost perfectly to the kind of productive resistance you want. ([arXiv][3])

**George Lewis, Voyager**
Use it as a model of partnership, not just interactivity. Independent internal process matters. So does nonhierarchy. ([EAMusic][7])

**AI companion harm literature + Ada Lovelace 2026**
Use this as a negative design spec. Avoid maximum-engagement optimization, endless affirmation, and relationship capture. Build for truth, contestability, and re-entry into the real world. ([arXiv][5])

**Traditions worth mining directly**

* IBIS / design rationale, for issue-centered collaboration
* Bakhtin, for polyphony
* Winnicott, for transitional space without reality confusion
* IFS, for parts with different agendas
* commonplace books / Zettelkasten, but only after you add real issue-state and contestation

The short version is:

**Keep** the membrane, salience competition, multi-signal retrieval, reread, and kernel authority over durable state.
**Loosen** the LLM boundary, context machinery, and operator surface.
**Demote** performance traversal, literal family ontology, and monolithic companion-self.
**Rebuild around** issue graphs, common ground, return tickets, taste, contestation, and polyphony.

If you keep optimizing for watchable daydreams, you’ll overbuild traversal and underbuild collaboration.
If you pivot around shared unresolved objects, the current kernel becomes a real asset instead of a historical monument.
